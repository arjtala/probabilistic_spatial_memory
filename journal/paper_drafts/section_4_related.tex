\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Episodic memory and look-back QA.}
Ego4D's Natural Language Queries
task~\cite{ego4d-nlq} is the closest published precedent for our
look-back QA framing: text query over an egocentric video, return a
$[t_\text{start}, t_\text{end}]$ interval. NLQ baselines treat the
entire video as a single bag of frames and rank with a learned
text-frame alignment; we use the same eval shape but report on
Nymeria (longer, mobile recordings with SLAM-derived spatial
metadata) and substitute a fixed CLIP encoder + a bounded-memory
spatial-temporal index for the trained NLQ models. The Ego-Exo4D
atomic-description protocol~\cite{ego-exo4d} provided the
narration-as-query inspiration for our atomic-action repurposing of
Nymeria.

\paragraph{Long-context video question answering.}
EgoSchema~\cite{egoschema} and MovieChat~\cite{moviechat} document
the temporal-grounding collapse that motivates our PSM-as-prefilter
pipeline: frontier MLLMs (Gemini, Claude, GPT) achieve strong
single-frame visual grounding but degrade sharply on temporal
localization over long-context video. MovieChat in particular
proposes a memory architecture (dense $\rightarrow$ sparse token
compression) for the long-video QA setting; the design point differs
from ours in two ways. MovieChat compresses tokens \emph{inside} an
MLLM forward pass, requiring re-running the model on the compressed
state; PSM compresses the substrate \emph{outside} the MLLM, returning
a bounded top-$K$ candidate set that any downstream MLLM can rerank
without retraining. The two approaches are complementary: PSM as
front-end retrieval, MovieChat-style memory as MLLM-internal
compression.

\paragraph{Streaming approximate-nearest-neighbor indices.}
DiskANN~\cite{diskann}, SPANN~\cite{spann}, and streaming variants
of HNSW~\cite{hnsw} address billion-scale ANN search with bounded
per-query cost. They are the right substrate when (a) the query is
purely a vector lookup with no spatial constraint, and (b) the index
is built once over a static or near-static corpus. PSM differs on
both axes: queries optionally constrain to an H3 $k$-ring
neighborhood (``what was I doing near here?''), and ingestion is
fully streaming with bounded per-cell state that ages out via the
ring buffer. PSM's reservoir-sampled exemplars per cell are
analogous to a tiny per-cell ANN index ($R \leq 1024$ per cell,
exact-cosine over $R$), and could in principle be replaced by a
small HNSW; we use exact cosine for the v1 evaluation because the
per-cell $R$ is small enough that the asymptotic ANN benefit does
not apply.

\paragraph{Spatial databases and geographic indexing.}
R-trees~\cite{guttman1984rtrees} and space-filling
curves~\cite{moon2001geohilbert} are the canonical spatial-database
substrates for $(x, y)$-keyed lookups. PSM's H3 tessellation~\cite{uber-h3}
is structurally a regular-grid alternative to R-tree's data-driven
subdivisions; H3's hexagonal geometry gives equidistant neighbors
(the basis for $k$-ring queries) and a globally-stable cell-id
scheme that supports federated-memory operations across devices
without coordinating a shared spatial schema. PSM is not a geo-database
in the SQL sense --- it does not support range, join, or aggregation
queries beyond ``top-$K$ by cosine similarity'' --- but inherits the
$O(\log)$ cell-lookup property of the underlying H3 index.
