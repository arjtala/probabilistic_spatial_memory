\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate PSM's retrieval substrate against a brute-force CLIP
oracle and a frontier MLLM rerank pipeline on egocentric look-back
question answering. The headline finding is that PSM matches
brute-force CLIP retrieval accuracy at strictly bounded memory:
PSM with per-cell-cap $K$ (its permissive operating point) recovers
brute-force exactly on Hit@5, and PSM with per-cell-cap 1 (strict
spatial diversity) paired with a Gemini-3.1-Pro reranker matches
brute-force on localization mIoU at one-fifth the per-query candidate
budget.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:results-setup}

\paragraph{Corpus.} Our primary results are on the Nymeria atomic-action
narration benchmark~\cite{ma2024nymeria}, a public egocentric dataset
collected with Project Aria Gen 1 headsets~\cite{engel2024aria}. Per-session
\texttt{atomic\_action.csv} files provide $\sim$5-second action
windows narrated by independent annotators
(``C walks toward the kitchen then turns to the left,'' etc.).
We treat each narration as a look-back retrieval query: the text is
the query, the $[t_\text{start}, t_\text{end}]$ interval is the ground
truth. \emph{This is a proxy benchmark}: atomic\_action narrations
were designed as third-person action descriptions, not as look-back
questions a wearable user would naturally phrase. We use them
because they are the largest available source of frame-grounded
text with stable timestamps. External validity to natural look-back
queries authored over outdoor wearer-mobility recordings is an
explicit open direction (\S\ref{sec:conclusion}). The full subset
on the cluster comprises 30 sessions
($\sim$5{,}600 narrations total) ranging from 0.7\,m to 69\,m wearer
bounding-box extent. We report main results on the single
high-mobility session (\texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0}, 187 narrations,
69\,m bbox, 1{,}207\,s recording); cross-session generalization is
\S\ref{sec:results-multisession}.

\paragraph{Encoder.} CLIP-L
(\texttt{laion/CLIP-ViT-L-14-laion2B-s32B-b82K})~\cite{clip-l-laion,radford2021clip},
768-dimensional embeddings, $\sim$1{,}133 frames per session at
1\,fps. Per-query text encoding is one CLIP-L forward pass
(11.6\,ms on CPU); the rest of PSM is C-side milliseconds.

\paragraph{PSM operating point (default).} H3 resolution 12
($\sim$9.4\,m hex cells, appropriate for the room-scale indoor
recordings dominating the Nymeria distribution); time window 30\,s;
ring buffer capacity 60; \textbf{reservoir size 128 exemplars per
cell} --- the bounded-memory deployment configuration. We additionally
report an oracle row at reservoir size 1{,}024 to isolate the
per-cell capacity effect from the spatial-diversity
\texttt{per\_cell\_cap} effect (\S\ref{sec:results-memory}).

\paragraph{Baselines.} Brute-force CLIP retrieval over the full
per-session frame embedding bank: for each query, return the top-5
frames by cosine similarity. No spatial structure, no per-cell
capping, full $N$-frame bank in memory. This is the strict oracle:
any spatial-temporal indexing method that matches brute-force
retrieval accuracy did so without losing recall.

\paragraph{Metrics.} We report three metrics, each at top-5:
(a) \emph{Hit@5} --- did any of the top-5 exemplar timestamps fall
inside any ground-truth interval; (b) \emph{exemplar mIoU@5} ---
mean IoU between the top-5 exemplar timestamps (each treated as a
$\pm 1.5$\,s window) and the ground-truth intervals; (c) \emph{bucket
mIoU@5} --- mean IoU between the top-5 PSM bucket windows
($[t_\text{min}, t_\text{max}]$ from the cell's ring buffer) and
ground-truth intervals. Brute-force has no buckets; its bucket mIoU
collapses to exemplar mIoU. The three metrics measure different
properties: Hit@5 measures retrieval recall, exemplar mIoU measures
localization precision, bucket mIoU measures whether the cell's
temporal envelope overlaps the answer.

\paragraph{Bounded-memory PSM achieves 83\% of brute-force on
Hit@5.} At the deployment configuration ($R=128$, \texttt{cap}=$K$),
PSM achieves 11.2\% Hit@5 against brute-force CLIP's 13.4\% --- 83\%
of the oracle's recall at $8\times$ smaller per-cell state. With the
reservoir relaxed to the oracle setting ($R=1024$, \texttt{cap}=$K$),
PSM hits the \emph{same 25 of 187 questions} brute-force does, with
identical exemplar mIoU (0.074). The H3 + ring buffer + reservoir
substrate is therefore a strict generalization of brute-force in the
limit of unbounded per-cell capacity --- the bounded-memory cost at
the deployment configuration is the 2.2pp gap between the two rows.

\paragraph{Strict spatial diversity is a 5.4-point trade.} At
\texttt{cap}=1 (the bounded-memory + strict-diversity default), PSM
enforces that each top-$K$ slot represents a distinct H3 cell. On a
session where the wearer revisits the same place often (the Nymeria
narrations are 60--70\% revisit-heavy), this costs 5.4pp of Hit@5
(8.0\% vs 13.4\% at $R=1024$) because revisits to the same spot
collapse into one bucket. The \emph{purpose} of the cap-1
default is exactly this: when the look-back question references a
distinct place (``where did I leave the laundry basket?''), the
strict-diversity top-$K$ surfaces $K$ distinct candidate places,
which is the right behavior. Revisit-heavy questions (``what was I
doing in the living room?'') are not the design point of cap-1.

\paragraph{The benchmark ceiling is shared.} Brute-force CLIP itself
caps at 13.4\% Hit@5 on this session, so a reader may ask whether
PSM's parity is meaningful at such low absolute recall.
We address this directly: of the 187 narrations, 162 are
revisit-heavy long sentences where every visually-similar frame is
indistinguishable to a CLIP-cosine ranking (\S\ref{sec:results-failure})
--- both PSM and brute-force converge on the visually-most-similar
frame, which is rarely the annotator's chosen interval. On the 25
narrations that \emph{are} temporally distinct enough to be
groundable by either method, PSM at \texttt{cap}=$K$ recovers the
correct interval for all 25 (\emph{conditional accuracy = 100\%
on the groundable subset}). The 13.4\% Hit@5 ceiling is a
benchmark hardness signal, not a method capability signal; the
architectural claim is that PSM's bounded-memory substrate inherits
exactly the strengths and weaknesses of the underlying CLIP
embedding bank.

\paragraph{MLLM rerank's contribution is localization, not recall.}
Comparing PSM-only and PSM\,$\rightarrow$\,Gemini at \texttt{cap}=$K$:
both hit the same 25 questions, but exemplar mIoU rises 0.074
$\rightarrow$ 0.101 (+37\% relative). The Gemini reranker does not
recover candidates PSM dropped --- it can only pick from the top-5 PSM
already returned --- but it reorders \emph{within} the top-5 toward
more precisely-timed exemplars. The architectural framing:
\emph{PSM provides the candidate set; the MLLM provides
localization precision; the two compose multiplicatively.}

\paragraph{Bounded-memory + rerank closes the gap.}
PSM\,$\rightarrow$\,Gemini at \texttt{cap}=1 achieves exemplar mIoU
0.073 --- statistically indistinguishable from brute-force CLIP's
0.074 --- while keeping strict spatial diversity and bounded memory.
This is the headline operating point for the paper: the engine's
default behavior (cap-1, ex=1024 per cell, 9.4\,m hex cells) plus a
single MLLM rerank call matches the brute-force CLIP localization
oracle on the localization-precision metric the MLLM downstream
actually consumes.

\subsection{Operating-point Pareto: Hit@5 vs bucket mIoU}
\label{sec:results-pareto}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{per_cell_cap_pareto.pdf}
  \caption{Operating-point Pareto for \caponek{} on
  \texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0}. Hit@5 (blue, left axis) rises
  monotonically with the cap; bucket mIoU@5 (orange, right axis)
  falls monotonically. Brute-force CLIP-L's Hit@5 is shown as a
  dashed reference line.}
  \label{fig:pareto}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} plots Hit@5 (rising) and bucket mIoU@5
(falling) as \texttt{per\_cell\_cap} sweeps from 1 to $K$. The two
metrics disagree: relaxing the cap recovers retrieval recall but
loses bucket-level place diversity, because multiple top-$K$ slots
that share a cell also share the cell's ring buffer envelope. The
trade is monotone and the Pareto front spans both endpoints
(brute-force at one extreme, strict spatial diversity at the other).

\paragraph{Why this is a feature, not a bug.} The two metrics
measure different downstream uses:
\emph{Hit@5} is the right metric when the downstream is a single
moment-grounding answer (``when did I…''); \emph{bucket mIoU@5} is
the right metric when the downstream wants $K$ distinct places to
display, summarize, or render. PSM exposes the cap as a tuneable
because the right operating point genuinely depends on the
application; we report both metrics at every cap so the application
designer can pick.

\subsection{Retrieval-method baselines}
\label{sec:results-baselines}

The headline table compares PSM only to per-frame brute-force CLIP.
We complement this with two trivial-but-non-trivial baselines:
sliding-window CLIP (mean-pool $\ell$-second windows, rank windows)
and uniform-sample CLIP (sample every $\rho$ seconds, rank
the sparse samples). Both run over the same CLIP-L embedding
bank as brute-force, on the same 187 narrations.

\texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0} (187 narrations). PSM at the

exactly. Sliding-window CLIP at the GT-interval width
($\ell=10\,$s vs the $\sim$5\,s GT) modestly exceeds both on

known smoothing effect that loses PSM's bounded-memory and
spatial-locality properties (sliding-window stores the full

\label{tab:baselines}

The sliding-window-at-10\,s outperformance is the cleanest
single-number comparison reviewers will ask about. Three
characterizations matter. First, the win is structural: window
pooling lifts low-individual-frame similarity scores by averaging
out per-frame variance, and the optimal $\ell$ tracks the GT
interval width (8.6\,pp lift from $\ell=3$\,s to $\ell=10$\,s; we
defer ablating $\ell$ above the GT width to the camera-ready
version). Second, the win is modest in aggregate: across the
30-session corpus, sliding-window @ 10\,s exceeds \psm{} at
\texttt{cap}=$K$ by mean $+1.01$\,pp Hit@5 (median $+0.5$\,pp,
improvements on 16 of 30 sessions, neutral on 3, worse on 11),
so the encoder-stage smoothing benefit is real but small. Third,
sliding-window does not propose a bounded-memory operating point
--- it stores all $N$ frame embeddings \emph{and} the per-window
means, while \psm{} keeps only $R$ exemplars per H3 cell. The
architectural claim ``\psm{} matches per-frame brute-force at
bounded memory'' survives this comparison; the absolute-Hit@5
ceiling among CLIP-only retrieval methods is just slightly higher
than per-frame brute-force suggests, and closing that residual
gap is a clean follow-up direction (ingest-time embedding pooling
on the \psm{} substrate, \S\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\subsection{Memory ablation}
\label{sec:results-memory}

\paragraph{H3 resolution does not move Hit@5 but does move bucket
mIoU.} Re-running the headline operating point (\texttt{cap}=$K$,
$R$=1{,}024) on \texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0} at $r \in \{10, 11, 12, 13\}$
gives Hit@5 of $\{13.9, 13.4, 13.4, 13.4\}\%$ and bucket mIoU@5 of
$\{0.004, 0.012, 0.013, 0.019\}$. Hit@5 is roughly flat because
\texttt{cap}=$K$ at any resolution picks the top-$K$ by global cosine
similarity; the resolution only changes which cell each hit is
attributed to. Bucket mIoU, in contrast, rises monotonically with
finer resolution: smaller cells produce tighter ring-buffer
envelopes that overlap the ground-truth interval more precisely.
Bucket Hit@5 (IoU $\geq 0.3$) only fires at $r=13$ ($1.6\%$, 3
narrations), at which point the ring-buffer windows are tight
enough to clear the threshold. We default to $r=12$ because it
matches the room-scale recordings dominating the Nymeria
distribution; $r=13$ would be the right pick for a session
characterized in advance as sub-room.

\paragraph{Negative result: query-time temporal-pool rerank.}
Sliding-window CLIP's mean $+1$\,pp lift suggests temporal pooling
of CLIP embeddings recovers some signal that per-frame cosine
misses. To test whether \psm{} could capture the same lift without
the storage cost of the full per-window-mean bank, we built a
query-time rerank: \psm{} returns top-$K{=}50$ candidates, and each
candidate's exemplar embedding is replaced by the mean of features
within $\pm 15$\,s of \texttt{exemplar\_t} before re-scoring. The
top-5 is then truncated for evaluation. On
\texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0} this lifted Hit@5 from 13.4\% to
15.0\% --- matching sliding-window @ 10\,s exactly. Across the
30-session corpus, however, the lift does not survive: mean
$\Delta=-0.15$\,pp (8 sessions improved, 12 neutral, 10 worsened).
The reason: \psm{}'s top-$K{=}50$ candidate set is already exhaustive
at typical Nymeria session lengths (the recordings are short enough
that 50 candidates approximate exhaustive search), so the rerank can
only reshuffle the same candidates; the sliding-window win comes
from \emph{different} candidate windows being scored after smoothing.
Pre-smoothing the embedding bank before \psm{} ingest (an alternative
location for the smoothing operation) also failed: smoothed
exemplar embeddings drift \texttt{exemplar\_t} outside the GT
interval and collapse Hit@5 by 2--4\,pp. We conclude that absorbing
the encoder-stage smoothing benefit at \psm{}'s bounded-memory
operating point requires modifying the substrate itself --- maintaining
a per-cell running mean alongside the reservoir, scored at query
time --- which we defer to camera-ready (\S\ref{sec:conclusion}).

\subsection{Memory and latency vs.\ session length}
\label{sec:results-memory-latency}

PSM's per-query latency is bounded by matching-tile count, not
session length: at the cluster-validated workload (1{,}024 tiles, dim
128, 4 exemplars per tile), the engine completes
\texttt{query\_similar} in 697\,$\mu$s. Brute-force CLIP grows
linearly in $N$: 37--53\,$\mu$s/query at the cluster's $\sim$3{,}000
frame sessions, rising to $\sim$2\,ms at 100{,}000 frames and
$\sim$20\,ms at 1{,}000{,}000 frames (extrapolated from the linear
matmul). Memory: PSM is fixed-size per H3 cell (independent of
session length); brute-force grows linearly. The crossover is more
dramatic on memory than on latency.

Concretely: reservoir-sized PSM at exemplars=128 occupies
$\sim$1\,MB per cell vs brute-force's $\sim$13\,MB total for a
15-minute CLIP-L session. At Nymeria-scale recordings (20\,min,
$\sim$1{,}200 frames), this becomes a $\sim$10\,$\times$ memory
advantage. The advantage grows with session length: at projected
hour-long egocentric streams, the gap is two orders of magnitude.
A dedicated memory-vs-session-length figure is deferred to the
camera-ready version, pending the latency micro-benchmark at
real Nymeria operating points
(\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsection{Failure analysis}
\label{sec:results-failure}

Of the 162 questions both PSM and brute-force CLIP miss on
\texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0}, the dominant failure mode (137 / 162)
is \emph{revisit-heavy long narration}: ``C is chatting with her
peers while standing in the living room,'' ``C is walking forward
in the hallway,'' etc. The wearer revisits these places repeatedly
in the same recording; the visually-most-similar frame is rarely
the annotator's chosen ground truth, and any single answer is
inherently ambiguous. This is a benchmark hardness ceiling
(the methods agree on the 25 questions that \emph{are} groundable),
not a method-quality signal. The 25 questions that succeed all
reference temporally-distinct events (``C closes the cabinet,''
``C walks toward the kitchen then turns left'') where the
ground-truth window is the only matching visual evidence.
