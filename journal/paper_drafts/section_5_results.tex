\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate \psm{} against a brute-force CLIP oracle, retrieval baselines, and a frontier MLLM on egocentric look-back QA.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:results-setup}

We evaluate on the Nymeria atomic-action benchmark~\cite{ma2024nymeria}, 30 sessions (${\sim}5{,}600$ narrations) collected with Project Aria Gen~1 headsets~\cite{engel2024aria}; each narration's $[t_\text{start}, t_\text{end}]$ interval serves as a look-back retrieval ground truth---a proxy benchmark, since the narrations are third-person action descriptions, not natural look-back questions (\S\ref{sec:conclusion}).
Primary results use \texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0} (187 narrations, 69\,m bbox).
The encoder is CLIP-L~\cite{clip-l-laion,radford2021clip}, 768-d, 1\,fps, 11.6\,ms per query on CPU.
\psm{} runs at H3 resolution~12 (${\sim}9.4$\,m hexes), time window 30\,s, ring-buffer capacity 60, reservoir $R{=}128$ exemplars per cell; we also report an oracle row at $R{=}1024$ to isolate reservoir eviction from the \caponek{} effect.
The baseline is brute-force CLIP top-5 over the full $N$-frame embedding bank (no spatial structure, no capping).
We report three top-5 metrics: Hit@5 (did any exemplar timestamp fall inside a GT interval), exemplar mIoU@5 (mean IoU between exemplar $\pm1.5$\,s windows and GT), and bucket mIoU@5 (mean IoU between each cell's $[t_\text{min}, t_\text{max}]$ envelope and GT).
Hit@5 measures retrieval recall; exemplar mIoU measures localization precision; bucket mIoU measures temporal-envelope overlap.

At the deployment configuration ($R{=}128$, \caponek{}$=K$), \psm{} reaches $83\%$ of brute-force Hit@5 ($11.2\%$ vs $13.4\%$) at $8\times$ smaller per-cell state.
At $R{=}1024$, \caponek{}$=K$ hits the \emph{same 25 of 187} questions with identical exemplar mIoU ($0.074$): the substrate strictly generalizes brute-force in the limit of unbounded capacity, with the bounded-memory cost being the $2.2$\,pp gap between the two reservoir sizes.
Strict spatial diversity (\caponek{}$=1$) costs $5.4$\,pp Hit@5 ($8.0\%$ vs $13.4\%$) because revisits to the same cell collapse into one bucket---the intended behavior for place-distinct queries; revisit-heavy questions are not the cap-1 design point.

Brute-force CLIP itself caps at $13.4\%$ Hit@5: 162 of 187 narrations are revisit-heavy and visually indistinguishable to CLIP cosine, consistent with the dimension-bounded retrieval limits of single-vector embeddings~\cite{weller2025limit} and with empirical evidence that CLIP maps distinct frames to ${>}0.99$ cosine similarity~\cite{li2025erroneous}.
On the 25 groundable narrations, \psm{} at \caponek{}$=K$ recovers all 25 (\emph{conditional accuracy ${=}100\%$}).
The MLLM rerank adds localization precision, not recall: exemplar mIoU rises $0.074\to0.101$ ($+37\%$ relative) while Hit@5 stays at 25/187.
At \caponek{}$=1$+rerank, exemplar mIoU $= 0.069$ is statistically indistinguishable from brute-force ($0.074$): paired bootstrap (10\,000 resamples) gives $\Delta\text{mIoU} = -0.005$, 95\% CI $[-0.032, +0.021]$.
The bounded-memory operating point matches the full embedding bank within sampling noise while preserving strict spatial diversity.

Four additional patterns from ablations and baselines:
(1)~\emph{Operating-point Pareto}: as \caponek{} sweeps from 1 to $K$, Hit@5 rises and bucket mIoU falls monotonically.
(2)~\emph{Retrieval baselines}: sliding-window CLIP @ $\ell{=}10$\,s exceeds \psm{} \caponek{}$=K$ by $+1.6$\,pp Hit@5 ($15.0\%$ vs $13.4\%$) by averaging out per-frame variance, but requires the full $N$-frame bank; uniform-sample forms the floor at $4.3$--$8.6\%$.
(3)~\emph{Memory ablation}: tightening the reservoir from 1024 to 128 exemplars costs $2.2$\,pp Hit@5 at \caponek{}$=K$; H3 resolution barely moves Hit@5 (${\pm}0.5$\,pp across $r \in \{10,11,12,13\}$) but moves bucket mIoU monotonically ($0.004 \to 0.019$).
(4)~\emph{30-session monotonicity}: across all 30 Nymeria sessions, mean Hit@5 climbs monotonically from $3.26\%$ at \caponek{}$=1$ through $5.69\%$ and $7.08\%$ to $8.92\%$ at \caponek{}$=K$, matching brute-force exactly at \caponek{}$=K$; sliding-window CLIP exceeds both by $+1.0$\,pp.

\subsection{Memory, latency, and failure modes}
\label{sec:results-memory-latency}

\psm{}'s per-cell storage at the deployment configuration ($R{=}128$ exemplars $\times$ 768-d $\times$ 4\,B $\approx 0.4$\,MiB per cell, plus ring buffer and HLL $\approx 1$\,MiB) is session-length-invariant; brute-force grows linearly in $N$.
Figure~\ref{fig:f6-memory} plots both: the brute-force = \psm{} crossover sits at $N \approx 4400$ frames (${\approx}73$\,min at 1\,fps, CLIP-L 768-d).
Past the crossover the gap diverges: $3.2\times$ at 4 hours, $6.4\times$ at 8 hours---a deployment-scale property.
In our measured corpus ($N \in [748, 1325]$), brute-force is cheaper (${\sim}3$\,MB vs ${\sim}13$\,MB at ${\sim}10$ cells per session); \psm{} pays its constant overhead before brute-force catches up.

Of the 162 questions both methods miss, most are revisit-heavy narrations where visually-similar frames recur across the session.
The 25 groundable questions all reference temporally-distinct events.
The shared $13.4\%$ ceiling is benchmark hardness, not method quality (\S\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsection{Multi-corpus, multi-encoder generalization}
\label{sec:results-multi-corpus}

To address the single-corpus / single-encoder objection, we replicate the spatial-axis result across 14 street-scale sessions spanning 3 sensor stacks (SLOPER4D~\cite{dai2023sloper4d}: head-mounted LiDAR + DJI Action2; Nymeria: Project Aria + MPS SLAM; LookOut~\cite{pan2025lookout}: Project Aria across 5 Bay Area locations) and 3 encoder families (CLIP-L 768-d, CLIP-bigG 1280-d, SigLIP-2-large 1024-d~\cite{tschannen2025siglip2}).
Acceptance criterion: for each (session, encoder), Hit@5 must be non-decreasing across $r \in \{10,11,12\}$ with $\geq 4$\,pp lift from $r{=}10$ to $r{=}12$; a session passes if any encoder satisfies both conditions.
The $4$\,pp threshold is conservative against binomial noise at 28--30 questions per session.

\subsection{Vanilla MLLM as retrieval: why MLLMs need a prefilter}
\label{sec:results-vanilla-mllm}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/f2_psm_vs_mllm.pdf}
\caption{Vanilla Gemini 3.1 Pro at $K{=}8$ uniform frames (red) vs \psm{} \texttt{clipL@r12} top-5 (blue) on every v1 session.
\psm{}'s query-guided retrieval reaches every session the uniform baseline misses; four sessions land at vanilla MLLM $= 0\%$ because the $K{=}8$ uniform frames never include a GT-interval frame.}
\label{fig:f2-psm-vs-mllm}
\end{figure}

We run Gemini 3.1 Pro on all 14 sessions at $K{=}8$ uniformly-sampled frames per question (Figure~\ref{fig:f2-psm-vs-mllm}).
The comparison isolates the retrieval substrate: given the same candidate budget, does query-guided cell-targeted retrieval provide candidates that uniform temporal sampling cannot?
On every session \psm{} matches or exceeds the MLLM's Hit@5, with a median ${\sim}4\times$ advantage.
Four sessions land at vanilla MLLM $= 0\%$ because the $K{=}8$ uniform frames never include a frame inside the ${\sim}3$\,s GT intervals---the structural coverage gap that \psm{}'s spatial-temporal prefilter is designed to close.

The root cause is temporal coverage: with session durations of 100--640\,s, the gap between uniform frames is 12--80\,s, yielding ${\sim}4\%$ probability of \emph{seeing} the right frame before any discrimination kicks in.
At $K{=}32$ (four times the deployment budget) the oracle upper bound climbs to $21.4\%$ but Gemini's actual pick rate stays at $7.1\%$---a $3\times$ discrimination gap on top of the coverage gap.
The single tie is Hillsdale6 at $13.8\%$, where \psm{} also has its weakest cross-encoder performance; notably \psm{} does not \emph{lose} on any session.

\subsection{PSM $\to$ MLLM rerank}
\label{sec:results-mllm-rerank}

The reranker permutes \psm{}'s top-5, so Hit@5 is invariant by construction.
On Hit@1 the effect is bimodal: it tripled Hit@1 on \texttt{seq008\_running\_001} ($10\%{\to}30\%$) and helped on \texttt{seq009} ($+3.6$\,pp), but hurt on \texttt{seq003} ($-3.3$\,pp) and Nymeria ($-1.6$\,pp).
The rerank is a visually-rich-query lever, useful when visual content discriminates beyond \psm{}'s embedding similarity, counterproductive on action-narrations.
The headline result---\psm{}'s query-guided retrieval reaching sessions that uniform sampling misses---is achievable without any MLLM in the loop; we do not promote the reranker to a default.
