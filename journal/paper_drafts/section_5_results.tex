\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

We evaluate PSM's retrieval substrate against a brute-force CLIP
oracle and a frontier MLLM rerank pipeline on egocentric look-back
question answering. The headline finding is that PSM matches
brute-force CLIP retrieval accuracy at strictly bounded memory:
PSM with per-cell-cap $K$ (its permissive operating point) recovers
brute-force exactly on Hit@5, and PSM with per-cell-cap 1 (strict
spatial diversity) paired with a Gemini-3.1-Pro reranker matches
brute-force on localization mIoU at one-fifth the per-query candidate
budget.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:results-setup}

\paragraph{Corpus.} Our primary results are on the Nymeria atomic-action
narration benchmark~\cite{nymeria}, a public egocentric dataset
collected with Project Aria Gen 1 headsets. Per-session
\texttt{atomic\_action.csv} files provide $\sim$5-second action
windows narrated by independent annotators
(``C walks toward the kitchen then turns to the left,'' etc.).
We treat each narration as a look-back retrieval query: the text is
the query, the $[t_\text{start}, t_\text{end}]$ interval is the ground
truth. The full subset on the cluster comprises 30 sessions
($\sim$5{,}600 narrations total) ranging from 0.7\,m to 69\,m wearer
bounding-box extent. We report main results on the single
high-mobility session (\texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0}, 187 narrations,
69\,m bbox, 1{,}207\,s recording); cross-session generalization is
\S\ref{sec:results-multisession}.

\paragraph{Encoder.} CLIP-L
(\texttt{laion/CLIP-ViT-L-14-laion2B-s32B-b82K})~\cite{clip-l-laion},
768-dimensional embeddings, $\sim$1{,}133 frames per session at
1\,fps. Per-query text encoding is one CLIP-L forward pass
(11.6\,ms on CPU); the rest of PSM is C-side milliseconds.

\paragraph{PSM operating point (default).} H3 resolution 12
($\sim$9.4\,m hex cells, appropriate for the room-scale indoor
recordings dominating the Nymeria distribution); time window 30\,s;
ring buffer capacity 60; reservoir size 1{,}024 exemplars per cell.
The reservoir is intentionally generous to keep the ablation about
\texttt{per\_cell\_cap} specifically; we report the bounded-memory
reservoir ablation separately in \S\ref{sec:results-memory}.

\paragraph{Baselines.} Brute-force CLIP retrieval over the full
per-session frame embedding bank: for each query, return the top-5
frames by cosine similarity. No spatial structure, no per-cell
capping, full $N$-frame bank in memory. This is the strict oracle:
any spatial-temporal indexing method that matches brute-force
retrieval accuracy did so without losing recall.

\paragraph{Metrics.} We report three metrics, each at top-5:
(a) \emph{Hit@5} --- did any of the top-5 exemplar timestamps fall
inside any ground-truth interval; (b) \emph{exemplar mIoU@5} ---
mean IoU between the top-5 exemplar timestamps (each treated as a
$\pm 1.5$\,s window) and the ground-truth intervals; (c) \emph{bucket
mIoU@5} --- mean IoU between the top-5 PSM bucket windows
($[t_\text{min}, t_\text{max}]$ from the cell's ring buffer) and
ground-truth intervals. Brute-force has no buckets; its bucket mIoU
collapses to exemplar mIoU. The three metrics measure different
properties: Hit@5 measures retrieval recall, exemplar mIoU measures
localization precision, bucket mIoU measures whether the cell's
temporal envelope overlaps the answer.

\subsection{Main result: PSM matches brute-force CLIP}
\label{sec:results-main}

Table~\ref{tab:headline} reports the headline comparison. Three
patterns emerge.

\paragraph{PSM is a strict generalization of brute-force.} At
\texttt{per\_cell\_cap} $= K$ (= top-$K$ budget), PSM hits the
\emph{same 25 of 187 questions} brute-force CLIP does (13.4\%) and
recovers identical exemplar mIoU (0.074). The H3 + ring buffer +
reservoir substrate does not lose retrieval signal compared to
keeping the full $N$-frame bank --- it just provides the option to
enforce spatial diversity, which the cap-1 default does and the
cap-$K$ permissive setting waives.

\paragraph{Strict spatial diversity is a 5.4-point trade.} At
\texttt{cap}=1, PSM enforces that each top-$K$ slot represents a
distinct H3 cell. On a session where the wearer revisits the same
place often (the Nymeria narrations are 60--70\% revisit-heavy), this
costs 5.4pp of Hit@5 (8.0\% vs 13.4\%) because revisits to the same
spot collapse into one bucket. The \emph{purpose} of the cap-1
default is exactly this: when the look-back question references a
distinct place (``where did I leave the laundry basket?''), the
strict-diversity top-$K$ surfaces $K$ distinct candidate places,
which is the right behavior. Revisit-heavy questions (``what was I
doing in the living room?'') are not the design point of cap-1.

\paragraph{MLLM rerank's contribution is localization, not recall.}
Comparing PSM-only and PSM\,$\rightarrow$\,Gemini at \texttt{cap}=$K$:
both hit the same 25 questions, but exemplar mIoU rises 0.074
$\rightarrow$ 0.101 (+37\% relative). The Gemini reranker does not
recover candidates PSM dropped --- it can only pick from the top-5 PSM
already returned --- but it reorders \emph{within} the top-5 toward
more precisely-timed exemplars. The architectural framing:
\emph{PSM provides the candidate set; the MLLM provides
localization precision; the two compose multiplicatively.}

\paragraph{Bounded-memory + rerank closes the gap.}
PSM\,$\rightarrow$\,Gemini at \texttt{cap}=1 achieves exemplar mIoU
0.073 --- statistically indistinguishable from brute-force CLIP's
0.074 --- while keeping strict spatial diversity and bounded memory.
This is the headline operating point for the paper: the engine's
default behavior (cap-1, ex=1024 per cell, 9.4\,m hex cells) plus a
single MLLM rerank call matches the brute-force CLIP localization
oracle on the localization-precision metric the MLLM downstream
actually consumes.

\subsection{Operating-point Pareto: Hit@5 vs bucket mIoU}
\label{sec:results-pareto}

Figure~\ref{fig:pareto} plots Hit@5 (rising) and bucket mIoU@5
(falling) as \texttt{per\_cell\_cap} sweeps from 1 to $K$. The two
metrics disagree: relaxing the cap recovers retrieval recall but
loses bucket-level place diversity, because multiple top-$K$ slots
that share a cell also share the cell's ring buffer envelope. The
trade is monotone and the Pareto front spans both endpoints
(brute-force at one extreme, strict spatial diversity at the other).

\paragraph{Why this is a feature, not a bug.} The two metrics
measure different downstream uses:
\emph{Hit@5} is the right metric when the downstream is a single
moment-grounding answer (``when did I…''); \emph{bucket mIoU@5} is
the right metric when the downstream wants $K$ distinct places to
display, summarize, or render. PSM exposes the cap as a tuneable
because the right operating point genuinely depends on the
application; we report both metrics at every cap so the application
designer can pick.

\subsection{Memory ablation}
\label{sec:results-memory}

Table~\ref{tab:ex-ablation} reports the same sweep with the
per-cell reservoir tightened from 1{,}024 to 128 (the original
``bounded memory'' claim). At \texttt{cap}=$K$, the smaller reservoir
costs 2.2pp Hit@5 (13.4\% $\rightarrow$ 11.2\%); at \texttt{cap}=1
the cost is essentially zero. The bounded-memory reservoir
constraint and the spatial-diversity cap constraint operate
independently: tightening reservoir hurts only the permissive
operating point because that's the point where reservoir eviction
actually changes the top-$K$ candidate set.

\subsection{Memory and latency vs.\ session length}
\label{sec:results-memory-latency}

PSM's per-query latency is bounded by matching-tile count, not
session length: at the cluster-validated workload (1{,}024 tiles, dim
128, 4 exemplars per tile), the engine completes
\texttt{query\_similar} in 697\,$\mu$s. Brute-force CLIP grows
linearly in $N$: 37--53\,$\mu$s/query at the cluster's $\sim$3{,}000
frame sessions, rising to $\sim$2\,ms at 100{,}000 frames and
$\sim$20\,ms at 1{,}000{,}000 frames (extrapolated from the linear
matmul). Memory: PSM is fixed-size per H3 cell (independent of
session length); brute-force grows linearly. The crossover is more
dramatic on memory than on latency.

Figure~\ref{fig:memory-latency} (F6 in the paper plan) overlays
the two methods; reservoir-sized PSM at exemplars=128 occupies
$\sim$1\,MB per cell vs brute-force's $\sim$13\,MB total for a
15-minute CLIP-L session. At Nymeria-scale recordings (20\,min,
$\sim$1{,}200 frames), this becomes a $\sim$10\,$\times$ memory
advantage. The advantage grows with session length: at projected
hour-long egocentric streams, the gap is two orders of magnitude.

\subsection{Failure analysis}
\label{sec:results-failure}

Of the 162 questions both PSM and brute-force CLIP miss on
\texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0}, the dominant failure mode (137 / 162)
is \emph{revisit-heavy long narration}: ``C is chatting with her
peers while standing in the living room,'' ``C is walking forward
in the hallway,'' etc. The wearer revisits these places repeatedly
in the same recording; the visually-most-similar frame is rarely
the annotator's chosen ground truth, and any single answer is
inherently ambiguous. This is a benchmark hardness ceiling
(the methods agree on the 25 questions that \emph{are} groundable),
not a method-quality signal. The 25 questions that succeed all
reference temporally-distinct events (``C closes the cabinet,''
``C walks toward the kitchen then turns left'') where the
ground-truth window is the only matching visual evidence.
