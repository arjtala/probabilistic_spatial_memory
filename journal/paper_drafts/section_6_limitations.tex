\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Benchmark ceiling on revisit-heavy narrations.}
Both \psm and brute-force CLIP plateau at 13.4\% Hit@5 on the
headline session (\S\ref{sec:results-main}).
The dominant failure mode is revisit-heavy long narrations:
162 of the 187 narrations describe rooms the wearer enters
many times during the recording, and each entry produces
visually near-identical frames.
Both methods converge on the most similar frame, which is
rarely the single interval the annotator chose as ground truth.
This is a benchmark hardness ceiling, not a method-quality
signal---\psm and brute-force CLIP agree on the 25 questions
that \emph{are} uniquely groundable and disagree only on the
inherently ambiguous remainder.
Future benchmarks should score against \emph{any} acceptable
revisit window rather than the one the annotator selected.

\paragraph{One honest spatial-axis failure.}
Of the 14 street-scale sessions in \S\ref{sec:results-multi-corpus},
\texttt{BurlingameDT4\_feb5} (LookOut, 242\,m bbox) is the only
session where all three encoders fail the monotone-\caponek lift
criterion.
The trajectory is a visually homogeneous parking-lot-flanked street
whose captioned queries collapse onto a handful of cells no encoder
discriminates above floor.
The same failure recurs on Aria Gen~2
Pilot's~\cite{aria-gen2-pilot} GPS-grounded outdoor walks
(300\,m extent, real GPS)---confirming the bottleneck is
encoder discrimination, not trajectory quality.

\paragraph{Latency from synthetic workload.}
\psm's reported ${\sim}700\,\mu$s per-query latency derives
from a 1024-tile, dim-128 synthetic CPU benchmark (single H200 host).
Real Nymeria sessions use ${\sim}$10 tiles at dim\,768 with
128 exemplars; the constants differ, and we have not yet run the
same benchmark harness directly on the Nymeria \psm{} state.
The actual-workload brute-force dot-product baseline is measured
separately (\S\ref{sec:results-memory-latency}), but the \psm{}
microsecond-scale number should be read as a systems sanity check,
not an on-device latency claim. The deployment target
is a wearable device (neither GPU nor H200-class CPU); porting the C
engine is straightforward but unmeasured here.

\paragraph{HLL utility not isolated.}
The HLL ring buffer supplies cardinality estimates and mergeable
time envelopes, but the present QA experiments score only retrieval
and localization. We do not ablate HLL against a cheaper
\texttt{(count,tmin,tmax)} cell summary, nor do we report cardinality
estimation error. The current evidence therefore justifies HLL as a
streaming mergeable substrate, not as an empirically necessary
component for Hit@5 on Nymeria.

\paragraph{No spatial-only baseline.}
Our baselines cover full-memory brute-force CLIP, sliding-window CLIP,
and uniform temporal sampling, but they do not include a spatial-only
heuristic such as nearest-GPS, random-cell, or low-visit-count cell
retrieval. The reported gains therefore establish that query-guided
cell retrieval beats uniform temporal coverage at the same candidate
budget; they do not fully isolate how much comes from spatial indexing
alone versus embedding similarity within the selected cells.

\paragraph{Multi-corpus coverage.}
The deepest single-session analysis
(Table~\ref{tab:headline}, the \caponek sweep, the
brute-force comparison) remains on Nymeria
\texttt{shelby\_arroyo\_act0}.
The multi-corpus sweep (\S\ref{sec:results-multi-corpus})
covers 14 sessions across three corpora and three encoder
families but reports Hit@5 only, under a heuristic monotonicity-plus-lift
criterion rather than a formal statistical test. mIoU and counting
metrics across the full 14-session set are a straightforward follow-up.
